Term Nasal epithelium

Epithelium lining the nose. Each nose chamber is divided into three regions: vestibule, respiratory segment and olfactory segment. The vestibule is lined with stratified squamous epithelium. Where the vestibule ends, the stratified squamous epithelium becomes thinner and undergoes a transition to the pseudostratified epithelium that characterizes the respiratory segment. The ciliated, pseudostratified columnar epithelium of the respiratory segment is composed of ciliated cells, goblet cells, brush cells, small granule cells and basal cells. The epithelium of the respiratory segment of the nasal cavity is essentially the same as the epithelium lining most of the parts that follow in the conducting system. The olfactory epithelium lining the olfactory segment of the nasal cavity, like the epithelium of the respiratory segment, is also pseudostratified but contains different cell types: bipolar neurons olfactory cells, supporting or sustentacular cells, basal cells and brush cells. The mucous lining of the nasal cavity, including lining of the nostril (vestibule) and the olfactory mucosa. Nasal mucosa consists of ciliated cells, goblet cells, brush cells, small granule cells, basal cells (stem cells) and glands containing both mucous and serous cells.
